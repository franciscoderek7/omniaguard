Context — Why This Matters

This Isn't About One Bad Day.
It's a National Tech Problem.

Canadian banks and telecoms are deploying AI-driven billing systems faster than their compliance teams can keep up. The result? Consumers get caught in automation errors with no clear path to resolution. Here's what's happening — and what you can do about it.

🏦

Merchant Pre-Authorization Abuse

Banks process merchant-initiated debits without adequate consent verification. When merchants charge incorrectly, dispute resolution is slow and consumer-hostile. Payments Canada Rule H1 requires immediate reversal of unauthorized PADs — most consumers don't know to ask for it.

📱

AI Billing Gaps in Telecom

Automated billing systems apply charges based on contract interpretations consumers never agreed to. The CCTS receives thousands of complaints annually — most involve billing errors that better AI guardrails would catch before they ever hit your account.

⚖️

Your Rights Are Stronger Than You Know

Most Canadians don't know they can legally record calls with companies under s.184(2)(a) of the Criminal Code. Or that CCTS rulings are binding. Or that unauthorized debits trigger mandatory bank investigation timelines. You deserve to know this.

🔐

PIPEDA & AI Decision Rights

When companies use AI to make billing decisions about your account, you have PIPEDA rights to know what data was used, how it was processed, and to contest automated decisions. Most companies don't proactively tell you. Now you know.

Case Study — Francisco v. Kudos / Telus / CIBC · 2026

The Full Timeline — With Your Legal Rights at Every Step

Every event below is documented and verifiable. Click any event to reveal your legal rights at that exact moment — so you know exactly what to do if this ever happens to you.

2026-05-31 · 09:00 EDT Banking
Unauthorized $823.21 Debit — CIBC Savings Account
Kudos Insurance initiated a debit of $823.21 from my CIBC savings account with no authorization and no valid pre-authorized debit agreement on file for this account type. Discovered on routine account check. Zero advance notice given.
Your Rights at This Moment
Payments Canada Rule H1: You have the right to dispute any unauthorized pre-authorized debit immediately. Your bank must acknowledge within 1 business day and complete investigation within 5 business days. Request an "Unauthorized PAD Declaration" form — this triggers a mandatory chargeback. You do NOT need to negotiate with the merchant first. FCAC regulations require same-day dispute acknowledgment. Your bank is legally obligated to act.
2026-05-31 · 11:30 EDT Telecom
Kudos Rep Admits on Record: "This Charge Should Not Have Gone Through"
Called Kudos customer service. Representative Tatiana acknowledged on a recorded call that the debit was applied to the wrong account and should not have processed. Admission captured under s.184(2)(a) Criminal Code participant recording doctrine.
Your Rights at This Moment
s.184(2)(a) Criminal Code — Participant Recording: As a party to the call, you have the absolute legal right to record it without notifying the other party. This recording is admissible evidence. The rep's admission is a vicarious admission binding on the corporation. Document: date, time, employee name, call reference number, exact words used. Request written confirmation of the admission within 72 hours. This acknowledgment alone is sufficient to support a chargeback demand.
2026-05-31 · 14:00 EDT Rights Concern
Manager Contradicts Admission — Denies Valid Claim
Escalated to manager. Manager directly contradicted Tatiana's admission, claimed the charge was "correctly processed," and refused to initiate reversal. Stated CIBC dispute "takes 30 days" — factually incorrect, and potentially designed to discourage the formal dispute process.
Your Rights at This Moment
Consumer Protection Act (Ontario) s.14: A false or misleading representation by a supplier is an unfair practice. Deliberately contradicting a prior agent admission to deny a valid dispute may constitute bad faith dealing. Request the manager's employee ID, note that this call is also recorded, and prepare to escalate to CCTS. Under Whiten v. Pilot Insurance (2002 SCC 18), bad-faith denial of a valid claim can attract punitive damages significantly exceeding the original amount in dispute.
2026-06-08 · Full Day Telecom
4.5-Hour Transfer Loop — Zero Resolution
Spent 4.5 hours across Kudos and Telus departments, transferred between agents multiple times, each denying jurisdiction. This "transfer loop" is a documented CCTS complaint pattern where companies create exhaustion barriers to discourage legitimate disputes from advancing. Every minute was logged and recorded.
Your Rights at This Moment
CCTS Complaint Eligibility: After a documented good-faith attempt to resolve with the provider (you now have this), file with the Commission for Complaints for Telecom-television Services at ccts-cprst.ca. Deliberate exhaustion tactics are a documented aggravating factor in CCTS proceedings. Under Mustapha v. Culligan (2008 SCC 27), quantifiable psychological distress from corporate conduct is compensable. Log every call: representative name, time, duration, what was said, what was refused.
2026-06-10 Legal Action
CCTS Complaint Filed — Case Open
Formal complaint filed with the Commission for Complaints for Telecom-television Services. CCTS has jurisdiction over Telus and Kudos billing disputes and can order reimbursement, compensation for inconvenience, and systemic remedies. Respondents have 20 business days to respond or CCTS investigation begins automatically.
CCTS Filing Guide
Visit ccts-cprst.ca — it's free and binding on member companies. You'll need: provider name, account number, issue description, what resolution you sought, and why it failed. Bring records of every contact. CCTS can award compensation for time, inconvenience, and financial harm. File within 1 year of the incident. The company must respond within 20 business days — non-response triggers automatic CCTS investigation.
2026-06-12 Legal Action
Civil Pre-Litigation Letter (CPL) Served — $321,332.84 Total Claim
Formal pre-litigation demand letter served to all parties. Total claim of $321,332.84 comprising actual damages, punitive damages for bad-faith denial, special damages for 29+ hours of professional time lost, psychological distress, and disgorgement of systemic overcharges. All claims supported by SCC authority.
Damages Framework
Actual: The $823.21 unauthorized debit.
Punitive (Whiten v. Pilot, 2002 SCC 18): Bad-faith denial of a valid claim can attract punitive damages of 10–30× the original amount where conduct is egregious and deliberate.
Special: Professional time lost documented at standard rate.
Distress (Mustapha v. Culligan, 2008 SCC 27): Provable psychological harm from prolonged corporate resistance.
Disgorgement (A.I. Enterprises v. AIM, 2014 SCC 12): Profits from unlawful conduct may be disgorged regardless of plaintiff's direct loss.
Ontario Small Claims covers up to $35,000. Superior Court for larger amounts — no cap.

Non-Response Consequences
Failure to respond to a CPL within the stated deadline demonstrates bad faith — admissible at trial. Non-response to CCTS within 20 business days triggers binding investigation. Every day of non-response is documented evidence of ongoing harm, which can increase distress and punitive components of the claim. CCTS non-engagement often results in a default finding in favour of the complainant.
