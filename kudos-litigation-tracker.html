2002 SCC 18
Whiten v. Pilot Insurance Co.
Punitive Damages — Bad Faith Conduct
The SCC upheld $1,000,000 in punitive damages against an insurer for bad faith, deliberate, high-handed conduct toward a vulnerable claimant. The Court confirmed that punitive damages are appropriate where the defendant's conduct is "harsh, vindictive, reprehensible and malicious." Kudos/Telus's conduct — unauthorized debit, false statements about email notice, 29+ hours of run-around while knowing Francisco's medical vulnerability and CPL deadline — meets this standard. The $823.21 debit and deliberate refusal to restore service despite Tatiana's admission are paradigm Whiten conduct.

Restitution — Recovery of Unlawfully Exacted Payments

Medical Vulnerability Module

Legal framework for enhanced damages

Francisco disclosed pre-existing medical conditions to Kudos/Telus agents during the call process. Under the thin skull / crumbling skull doctrine and Mustapha v. Culligan (2008 SCC 27), a tortfeasor takes the victim as they find them. Once medical vulnerability is disclosed and acknowledged, the foreseeability of psychiatric harm becomes actual rather than constructive — dramatically strengthening aggravated and psychiatric injury claims.

Disclosure Made

Francisco explicitly disclosed pre-existing medical conditions to Kudos/Telus during recorded calls. The company had actual knowledge of medical vulnerability before continuing its abusive conduct.

Stress Amplification

Unauthorized debit + internet shutdown + 29+ hours of run-around + CPL deadline + lost job + business closures = compounding stressors directly amplifying pre-existing conditions.

Foreseeable Harm

Under Mustapha v. Culligan, harm must be foreseeable to a person of ordinary fortitude OR the plaintiff's vulnerability was known. Here, vulnerability was disclosed — making harm actually foreseeable.

Thin Skull Rule

Even if the standard consumer wouldn't suffer psychiatric injury, Kudos/Telus knew Francisco had medical vulnerabilities. They cannot shelter behind the ordinary fortitude standard once actual vulnerability is known.

Recommended Evidence to Secure

1. Medical records documenting pre-existing conditions (obtain from physician).
2. Doctor's note linking stress caused by Kudos/Telus conduct to symptom aggravation.
3. Timestamps from recordings where disclosure was made to specific Kudos/Telus agents.
4. Any pharmacy records, appointment records, or hospital visits following May 31 incident.
5. Affidavit detailing subjective experience of mental suffering and its connection to the unauthorized debit and subsequent 29+ hour run-around.
