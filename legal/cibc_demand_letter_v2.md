## IV. LEGAL ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Negligence

CIBC owed Mr. Francisco a duty of care as a banking customer: *Cooper v. Hobart*, 2001 SCC 79 (two-stage Anns test: proximate relationship + no policy reasons to negate duty). As Mr. Francisco's bank, CIBC assumed responsibility for providing accurate information about account protection and actioning reasonable customer requests.

**Breach:** The agent's statement that CIBC had "no mechanism" to block Kudos was confirmed by the Manager (June 8) to be incorrect. This is a breach of the standard of care applicable to a financial institution providing information to a customer about their account security. The four disconnections without resolution compound the breach.

**Causation:** But for CIBC's failure to act on Mr. Francisco's request to block Kudos, the unauthorized debit and all consequential harm would not have occurred.

**Damages:**
- Unauthorized PAD debit: $45
- Resulting fees/charges (documented): $[amount from records]
- 24+ hours resolving (reasonable compensation for time): $2,400
- **Subtotal negligence:** ~$2,500+

### 4.2 Negligent Misrepresentation

Elements per *Queen v. Cognos Inc.*, [1993] 1 SCR 87:

1. **Special relationship giving rise to duty of care:** CIBC-customer banking relationship — **established**
2. **Representation was untrue, inaccurate, or misleading:** "No mechanism" to block Kudos PAD — confirmed false by Manager's June 8 admission that agent gave wrong information — **established**
3. **Representor acted negligently:** Agent failed to verify available PAD blocking options before advising customer — **established**
4. **Representee reasonably relied:** Mr. Francisco could not take alternative protective steps based on agent's advice — **established**
5. **Reliance caused detriment:** Kudos continued debiting; harm flowed — **established**

All five elements are met. This is the core legal claim.

**Damages:** $25,000 (loss flowing from reliance on incorrect banking advice, including consequential harm from inability to block unauthorized debit)

### 4.3 Fraudulent Misrepresentation — ATM Referral Advertisement

CIBC's ATMs display "Refer a friend, get $75" to approximately 11 million customers. The advertisement does not disclose a material prerequisite: the referred friend must make a $500 minimum deposit within a specified period.

Elements as settled in Canadian common law:

1. **False representation:** Advertisement displays $75 bonus without disclosing $500 deposit prerequisite — a material omission — **established**
2. **Made knowingly or recklessly:** CIBC designed, approved, and deployed the advertisement with full knowledge of its terms — **established**
3. **Intent to induce:** Advertisement is designed to induce customers to make referrals — **established**
4. **Reliance:** Mr. Francisco referred John Ma Fusco in reliance on the advertisement — **established**
5. **Damage:** Mr. Francisco waited 150 days; bonus was denied; referral strained the relationship with the referred friend — **established**

**Damages:** $2,500 (denial of bonus + consequential relational and reputational damage from referring a friend to a product that was not as advertised)

### 4.4 Disability Discrimination — Ontario Human Rights Code

Sections 1, 5, and 11 of the *Ontario Human Rights Code* prohibit discrimination in the provision of services on the basis of disability and impose a duty to accommodate to the point of undue hardship.

**Direct discrimination (s.5):** After Mr. Francisco disclosed his disability:
- The Agent (May 15) provided no alternative to online processes
- The Manager (June 8) disconnected without providing accommodation
- The Supervisor (June 15) disconnected without providing accommodation
- The Manager/Supervisor #2 (June 15) directed him to "apply online" — without assessing whether this was an accessible alternative — and then disconnected

Four disconnections following disability disclosure constitutes a pattern of discriminatory treatment in the provision of services.

**Failure to accommodate (s.11):** CIBC failed to inquire into the nature of Mr. Francisco's disability or offer any alternative service pathway. Directing a disabled customer to "apply online" without first determining whether this is accessible constitutes constructive discrimination.

**Note:** Mr. Francisco is concurrently filing an application with the Human Rights Tribunal of Ontario. This letter does not constitute an election of remedies and does not waive HRTO rights.

**Damages:** $15,000 (injury to dignity, feelings, and self-respect; failure to accommodate; aggravation of pre-existing conditions from repeated discriminatory treatment)

### 4.5 Medical Aggravation — Thin Skull Principle

Per *Athey v. Leonati*, [1996] 3 SCR 458, a tortfeasor takes a plaintiff as they find them. CIBC's conduct aggravated Mr. Francisco's pre-existing medical conditions. He was left without food and medicine while spending 24+ hours on calls over a 30-day period.

**Damages:** $10,000 (with supporting medical records; adjusted upon production of documentation)

### 4.6 Breach of Implied Contractual Terms

The bank-customer contract contains implied terms including that CIBC will:
- Provide accurate information about account protection mechanisms
- Take reasonable steps to protect accounts from unauthorized third-party debits when requested
- Provide accessible customer service to disabled customers

CIBC breached all three implied terms.

**Damages:** $10,000

### 4.7 Punitive Damages

*Whiten v. Pilot Insurance*, 2002 SCC 18 establishes that punitive damages are appropriate where a defendant's conduct is "harsh, vindictive, reprehensible, and malicious" and where compensatory damages alone do not sufficiently deter or denounce the conduct.

CIBC's conduct — while not at the level of *Whiten*'s multi-year campaign to frame a widow for arson — exhibits a pattern of institutional bad faith:
- Systematic refusal to assist on all four occasions
- Identical "no mechanism" response from four separate employees, evidencing a systemic institutional policy
- Four disconnections of a disabled customer who disclosed his disability
- Manager offering $40 while admitting fault, then disconnecting
- Failure to preserve a documented complaint over 30 days

This pattern warrants punitive damages to deter CIBC from applying the same systemic "no mechanism" policy to other disabled customers in similar situations.

**Punitive damages claimed:** $100,000

Note: *Whiten*'s $1M punitive was exceptional and involved far more egregious conduct. The $100,000 claimed here is proportionate to CIBC's documented conduct and the need for deterrence without overclaiming.

## V. COMPETITION ACT REGULATORY NOTICE

Mr. Francisco provides notice of intent to file the following regulatory complaints if this matter is not resolved:

### 5.1 False/Misleading Representations — s.52 (Criminal) and s.74.01 (Civil)

CIBC's ATM advertisement constitutes a false or misleading representation as to the $75 referral bonus by failing to disclose the material $500 deposit prerequisite. This is actionable under both the criminal stream (s.52, up to 14 years) and the civil stream (s.74.01, up to $10M per count).

### 5.2 Bait-and-Switch — s.74.04

CIBC advertised a $75 bonus and then failed to supply it based on a condition not disclosed in the advertisement. This meets the elements of bait-and-switch selling under s.74.04.

### 5.3 Drip Pricing — s.74.1 (2024 Amendments, effective June 2024)

The $500 deposit prerequisite, not disclosed at the point of advertisement, constitutes a material hidden condition under the 2024 drip pricing amendments.

**Note on exposure:** Mr. Francisco estimates 1% of CIBC's 11 million customers made referrals under this advertisement, potentially affecting 110,000 individuals. At $75 per person, total undisclosed exposure exceeds $1M. This matter will be referred to the Competition Bureau of Canada.
