**The Core Event: Internet Service Terminated on the Basis of an Unauthorized Third-Party Charge**

Telus terminated my internet service on the basis of a **$45.00 charge** that was placed in my name without my authorization. This charge originated from a Kudos-related billing error — not from any amount I owed Telus directly.

The consequences of this service disruption were not minor inconveniences. They were serious, cascading, and professionally catastrophic:

**1. Interference with Active Litigation — $38.3 Million in Proceedings**

At the time of Telus's service termination, I had active litigation with a combined value of approximately **$38.3 million**. Court filings required internet access. Legal research required internet access. Communications with parties, counsel, and registrars required internet access. The disruption of my internet service — caused by a Kudos billing error, not any default on my part — directly impaired my ability to advance these proceedings.

Under *Fisher v. Knibbe*, 1992 ABCA, interference with a party's ability to participate meaningfully in their own litigation is a compensable head of damages. This was not a missed convenience. This was interference with access to justice.

**2. Disruption to 60+ Business Operations**

I operate a portfolio of 60+ companies under Francisco Holdings Inc. Virtually every aspect of this business — communications, contracting, financial management, client relationships — depends on reliable internet connectivity. Termination of service on the basis of an unauthorized third-party charge caused business interruption across this entire portfolio.

Under *De Rita v. 1266078 Ontario Inc.*, 2024 ONCA, loss of business opportunity arising from another party's negligence or wrongful conduct is recoverable in full.

**3. Medical Consequences — Thin Skull Rule**

During the period of this disruption and the 24+ hours spent on combined Kudos/Telus calls attempting to resolve it, I was left without food, without medication, and without access to the resources needed to manage my documented medical conditions. These conditions were directly aggravated as a result.

Under *Athey v. Leonati* [1996] 3 SCR 458, a tortfeasor takes their victim as they find them. Where a pre-existing condition is aggravated by the defendant's wrongful conduct, the defendant is liable for the full extent of the aggravated harm — including consequences that would not have affected a person without the pre-existing condition.

**4. The Pattern of Abandonment**

The conduct of Telus and its partner Kudos, in combination, reflects a pattern of institutional abandonment that compounded every harm described above:

- Manager **Glen** promised a callback within 48 hours — he never called;
- **Sherry** called in Glen's place but provided no resolution;

Telus, as the network provider and infrastructure layer for this partner relationship, bears joint accountability for the conduct that occurred within its ecosystem.
