> **DRAFTING NOTES (remove before filing):**
> 1. This draft prices the claim at **$150,000** (matching your settlement offer) plus punitive/aggravated damages, **not** the $8.2M–$13.2M figure. Courts can strike or sanction claims with damages that bear no rational relationship to the pleaded facts. A $150K claim backed by recorded admissions is strong. A $13M claim from an $823 debit invites a motion to strike and damages your credibility on the parts that are real.
> 2. **"Constitutional Interference" and standalone "Human Rights Discrimination" causes of action are dropped.** The Charter binds government actors, not private telecom companies (s.32). Disability discrimination goes to the HRTO — it's in your regulatory complaint package, not here. Including it here as a civil cause of action is very likely to be struck.
> 3. **"Abuse of Process" is dropped** — that tort requires an existing legal proceeding misused for an improper purpose. Nothing here describes that.
> 4. **"Unconscionability" is pleaded as a basis to set aside contract terms, not as a standalone damages claim** — that's how it actually works (see *Uber v. Heller*, below).
> 5. **Individual defendants (Norman, Tatiana, Hector, Glenn):** Naming frontline employees personally is legally possible for intentional torts (fraud, conversion) but practically rare — they likely have no assets to satisfy judgment, and naming them invites personal cross-claims and litigation complexity. They're included below in the factual narrative as you instructed; consider before filing whether to sue them personally or rely on vicarious liability against their employer alone.
> 6. **Authority check on your SCC list:**
>    - *Whiten v. Pilot Insurance*, 2002 SCC 18 — fits, kept (punitive damages).
>    - *A.I. Enterprises Ltd. v. Bram Enterprises Ltd.*, 2014 SCC 12 — fits, kept (unlawful means / intentional interference with economic relations).
>    - *Garland v. Consumers' Gas Co.*, 2004 SCC 25 — fits, kept (unjust enrichment test).
>    - *Mustapha v. Culligan*, 2008 SCC 27 — fits, kept (psychiatric injury threshold).
>    - *1688782 Ontario Inc. v. Maple Leaf Foods Inc.*, 2020 SCC 35 — tangential (duty of care in commercial relationships); kept as supporting authority only.
>    - *Kingstreet Investments v. New Brunswick*, 2007 SCC 1 — about restitution for **unconstitutional government** exactions. Doesn't fit a private company billing dispute. **Recommend dropping.**
>    - *Canada (AG) v. PHS Community Services*, 2011 SCC 44 — Charter s.7 case about government conduct (Insite). Doesn't fit private defendants. **Recommend dropping.**
>    - *Uber Technologies Inc. v. Heller*, 2020 SCC 16 — fits well for unconscionability of standard-form contract terms. Kept.
>    - **Added:** *Bhasin v. Hrynew*, 2014 SCC 71 — duty of honest performance in contractual relationships. This is directly on point for Koodo's conduct and strengthens the breach of contract claim. Recommend keeping.

### MATERIAL FACTS

**The Equipment and Billing Misrepresentation**

6. The plaintiff entered into an agreement with Koodo Mobile under which equipment was represented as "rental equipment at no cost."

7. Contrary to this representation, the plaintiff was charged a hookup fee of $490.00 on equipment that was represented as free of charge.

8. The defendant Norman, acting as the defendants' sales representative, processed this transaction and, in doing so, violated the defendants' own internal policy by providing a connected device without an active plan.

9. The defendant Norman required the plaintiff, a person with a disclosed disability, to travel in person to a retail location to address an error that originated with the defendants' own billing system.

**The Unauthorized Debit**

10. On or about the date particularized in the evidence index appended hereto, the defendants caused an unauthorized debit of $823.21 to be withdrawn from the plaintiff's bank account, without the plaintiff's authorization and without prior notice.

11. A manager employed by the defendants subsequently represented to the plaintiff that email notification of this withdrawal had been sent to him in advance.

12. This representation was false. The plaintiff conducted a thorough search of all email accounts associated with him and confirmed that no such notification exists or was ever sent.

**The Stopped Cheque**

13. The defendants issued a cheque to the plaintiff in the amount of $490.00, and subsequently stopped payment on that cheque, the full circumstances of which are particularized in the evidence index appended hereto.

**The Admissions**

14. The defendants' representative, Tatiana, acknowledged on a recorded call — recorded lawfully by the plaintiff as a participant to the conversation pursuant to s.184(2)(a) of the *Criminal Code* — that the billing errors described above had occurred and were the result of error on the part of the defendants and their representatives.

**The Pattern of Abandonment**

15. Following the admissions described above, the plaintiff was subject to a sustained pattern of unfulfilled commitments and unreturned contact by the defendants' representatives, including but not limited to:

    (a) A missed scheduled callback;
    (b) Three separate instances in which a 48-hour callback commitment was made and not honoured;
    (c) On or about June 19, 2026, a representative identified as Hector stated "I wish I could help" and discontinued the interaction without resolution;
    (d) Later the same day, a callback was placed and the call was terminated without resolution;
    (e) The plaintiff was placed in a recurring callback loop without resolution.

16. This pattern of conduct is particularized further in the Abandonment Log appended to this claim as an exhibit.

**Service Disruption**

17. As a consequence of the unresolved billing dispute described above, the defendants caused disruption to the plaintiff's internet and/or mobile service, which in turn interfered with the plaintiff's ability to conduct business operations and to participate in ongoing legal proceedings requiring internet access.

**Damages Arising**

18. As a direct and foreseeable result of the conduct described above, the plaintiff has suffered:

    (a) Direct financial loss in the amount of $1,358.21 (being $823.21 + $490.00 + $45.00, particularized in the evidence index);
    (b) Loss of time, in excess of 29 hours, spent attempting to resolve these matters with the defendants;
    (c) Aggravation of pre-existing medical conditions, including periods without access to necessary medication, as a foreseeable consequence of the prolonged and unresolved dispute;
    (d) Interference with the plaintiff's business operations and ongoing legal proceedings; and
    (e) Mental distress arising from the defendants' conduct, particularly the pattern of admitted error followed by repeated unfulfilled commitments.

### CAUSES OF ACTION

**A. Breach of Contract**

19. The defendants breached their agreement with the plaintiff by charging fees on equipment represented as free of charge, contrary to the express terms of the agreement.

20. Further, pursuant to the duty of honest contractual performance recognized by the Supreme Court of Canada in *Bhasin v. Hrynew*, 2014 SCC 71, the defendants breached their duty to deal honestly with the plaintiff in the performance of the contract, including by way of the false representation regarding email notification described at paragraph 12.

**B. Fraudulent Misrepresentation**

21. The representation described at paragraph 11 — that email notification of the $823.21 withdrawal had been sent — was false, was made by the defendants' representative with knowledge of its falsity or with reckless indifference to its truth, was intended to be relied upon, and was relied upon by the plaintiff to his detriment.

**C. Conversion**

22. The defendants' withdrawal of $823.21 without authorization, and retention of funds associated with the stopped cheque described at paragraph 13, constitute wrongful interference with the plaintiff's property in those funds.

**D. Unjust Enrichment**

23. The defendants have been enriched by the unauthorized retention of the sums described above; the plaintiff has suffered a corresponding deprivation; and there is no juristic reason for the defendants' retention of these funds, applying the test set out in *Garland v. Consumers' Gas Co.*, 2004 SCC 25.

**E. Intentional Interference with Economic Relations**

24. The defendants' disruption of the plaintiff's internet service, in the context of an unresolved billing dispute and without adequate verification of the underlying charge, constitutes use of unlawful means directed at the plaintiff that foreseeably caused economic harm, applying the framework in *A.I. Enterprises Ltd. v. Bram Enterprises Ltd.*, 2014 SCC 12.

**F. Negligence**

25. The defendants owed the plaintiff a duty of care in the administration of his account and the handling of his billing dispute. The defendants breached this duty through the conduct particularized above, applying the proximity and foreseeability framework discussed in *1688782 Ontario Inc. v. Maple Leaf Foods Inc.*, 2020 SCC 35.

**G. Negligent Infliction of Mental Suffering**

26. The defendants' conduct was such that psychiatric or psychological harm to the plaintiff was a reasonably foreseeable consequence, applying the threshold set out in *Mustapha v. Culligan of Canada Ltd.*, 2008 SCC 27. The plaintiff pleads that this threshold is met by the cumulative effect of the conduct described above, and will provide particulars and any supporting medical evidence in due course.

**H. Unconscionability — Contract Terms**

27. To the extent the defendants rely on any standard-form contractual term — including any arbitration or limitation clause — to resist this claim, the plaintiff pleads that any such term is unconscionable and unenforceable in the circumstances, applying *Uber Technologies Inc. v. Heller*, 2020 SCC 16.

**I. Punitive and Aggravated Damages**

28. The defendants' conduct, taken as a whole — including the admitted billing error, the false representation regarding email notification, and the repeated pattern of unfulfilled commitments described in the Abandonment Log — was high-handed, reprehensible, and departed markedly from ordinary standards of decent business conduct, warranting an award of punitive damages in accordance with the principles set out in *Whiten v. Pilot Insurance Co.*, 2002 SCC 18, in addition to aggravated damages reflecting the manner in which the wrongs were committed.
